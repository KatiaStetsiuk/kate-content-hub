### Angle

McKinsey data shows only **5.5% of companies** generate significant value from AI — despite nearly 80% claiming regular AI use. That gap is not a technology problem. It's a capital allocation failure hiding in plain sight.

### Why It Works for Kate

Kate's entire positioning is AI as an economic and organisational question, not a technology one. This McKinsey finding validates her core thesis with hard data: adoption ≠ impact. She can layer in the OKKO case ($12M saved annually) as the counter-example — proof that the 5.5% isn't magic, it's method.

No other commentator on this data will frame it as a *capital allocation problem* with a real implementation anchor. That's Kate's unique angle.

### Opening Hook

> Most companies using AI have nothing to show for it. McKinsey just confirmed it: only 5.5% of organisations generate significant value from AI — yet nearly 80% say they use it regularly. That gap is not a technology problem. It's a decision-making problem at the top.

## Raw McKinsey Signal

| Finding | Data Point |
|---|---|
| Companies generating significant AI value | 5.5% |
| Companies reporting regular AI use | ~80% |
| High performers spending >20% digital budget on AI | >1/3 |
| High performers vs others on big AI bets | 5× more likely |

**Kate's counter-evidence**: OKKO — $12M annually, 2,350h/month. Not because they had better technology. Because they treated AI as a capital deployment decision and redesigned operations around it.
