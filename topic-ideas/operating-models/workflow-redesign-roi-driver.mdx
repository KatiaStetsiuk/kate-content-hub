### Angle

McKinsey's 2025 data identifies **workflow redesign** as the single biggest predictor of AI EBIT impact — and high performers are **nearly 3× more likely** to fundamentally redesign their workflows when deploying AI. The implication for CEOs: if your AI programme isn't touching your operating model, it isn't generating returns.

### Why It Works for Kate

This is Kate's thesis stated in McKinsey's language. Her positioning is that most AI fails because of *organisational design*, not technology. McKinsey just handed her the empirical anchor for that argument: workflow redesign is not a nice-to-have — it's the variable that separates the 5.5% from the 94.5%.

She can add the implementation layer McKinsey doesn't provide: what workflow redesign actually looks like in a real organisation, what governance decisions it requires, and why most leadership teams avoid it (it's harder than buying software).

### Opening Hook

> McKinsey just identified the #1 predictor of AI ROI. It's not which model you use. It's not how much you spend on infrastructure. It's whether your leadership team was willing to redesign how work actually gets done.

## Raw McKinsey Signal

| Finding | Data Point |
|---|---|
| High performers redesigning workflows | ~3× more likely than peers |
| Workflow redesign as EBIT predictor | Cited as the biggest single factor |
| Senior leadership ownership at high performers | 3× stronger engagement |
| High performers spending >20% of digital budget on AI | >1/3 of them |

**Kate's implementation layer**: Workflow redesign doesn't happen without governance decisions about who owns AI outcomes, who has authority to change processes, and how AI performance gets measured. The companies that redesign successfully have answered those questions before they deploy. The ones that haven't are buying tools and waiting for results that don't come.
