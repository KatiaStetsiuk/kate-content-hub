# Content Format Guides

## LinkedIn Post Formats

LinkedIn is Kate's primary channel. Every post should serve one of three goals:
- **Authority**: Establish expertise, build trust
- **Activation**: Generate comments (for follow-up outreach)
- **Conversion**: Drive AI Discovery / AI Opportunity Call interest

### Format A: The Provocation Post (best for activation)
Ideal for: challenging common beliefs, sparking debate, getting comments
Length: 150–250 words

```
[Bold contrarian opening — 1 sentence that challenges a common belief]

[2-3 lines expanding the provocation with evidence or observation]

Here's what I've seen instead: [Kate's real experience / OKKO / client example]

[3-5 bullet points or short paragraphs with the counter-argument]

[Closing position — Kate's clear POV, not a question]

[Optional soft CTA: "If this resonates, I run AI Opportunity Calls for exactly this situation."]
```

### Format B: The Case Study Snapshot (best for authority + conversion)
Ideal for: sharing OKKO or other results, proving ROI at executive level
Length: 200–300 words

```
[Headline stat or result — make it concrete and financially framed]

[2-3 lines on the company, the problem, and the stakes]

What we actually did:
→ [step/solution 1 — described as a strategic/operational decision, not a technical one]
→ [step/solution 2]
→ [step/solution 3]

The result: [specific numbers — time saved, money saved, efficiency gained, ROI]

[1 paragraph on the underlying lesson for other CEOs — frame as operating model or economics insight]

[Soft CTA: "If you're wondering whether something similar is possible in your organisation, 
that's exactly what an AI Discovery engagement maps out. Happy to talk."]
```

### Format C: The Insight Post (best for thought leadership)
Ideal for: sharing a sharp observation from the field, educating without selling
Length: 150–250 words

```
[Open with a surprising or counterintuitive observation]

[Short story or scenario that illustrates it — 3-5 lines]

The real issue: [name the underlying problem clearly]

[2-3 concrete takeaways or principles]

[End with Kate's conviction — a strong closing statement, not a question]
```

### Format D: The List Post (best for reach and saves)
Ideal for: actionable frameworks, checklists, "signs you're ready for X"
Length: 200–350 words

```
[Hook: "X signs your AI project is already failing" / "X questions every CEO should ask before investing in AI"]

[Brief 2-line intro establishing why this matters]

1. [Point with 1-2 sentence explanation]
2. [Point with 1-2 sentence explanation]
3. [Point with 1-2 sentence explanation]
[4-7 items total]

[Closing paragraph tying it together with Kate's POV]

[Optional CTA]
```

## Short Article Format (800–1,200 words)
Best for: Dataconomy, LinkedIn Articles, LyraTech blog

```
Headline: [Specific, bold, makes a promise or challenges a belief]
Subheadline: [Adds context or specificity]

[Opening paragraph: A surprising stat, observation, or scenario — 3-5 lines]

## [Section 1: The Problem / What Companies Get Wrong]
[300-400 words — be specific, use examples, challenge conventional thinking]

## [Section 2: What Actually Works / The Framework or Insight]
[300-400 words — Kate's methodology, case evidence, concrete principles]

## [Section 3: How to Apply This / What to Do Next]
[200-300 words — actionable, specific, honest about complexity]

[Closing paragraph: Strong POV, no hedging, optional CTA]

[Author bio: Kate Stetsiuk, CEO of LyraTech AI...]
```

## Long-form Article Format (2,000–3,500 words)
Best for: Forbes Ukraine, Dataconomy deep dives, gated content

Structure:
1. **Hook** (150–200 words): Surprising stat or provocative scenario
2. **The Problem in Depth** (400–600 words): Why this matters now, what's broken
3. **Common Mistakes** (300–500 words): What companies get wrong — be specific
4. **The Framework / Solution** (600–900 words): Kate's approach, with case evidence
5. **Case Study Section** (300–500 words): OKKO or another real example with numbers
6. **Practical Next Steps** (300–400 words): Concrete, actionable
7. **Closing** (150–200 words): Strong conviction, soft CTA

## Voice Rules (Apply to All Formats)

Kate speaks as a trusted strategic advisor who has sat in the boardroom, delivered real results,
and has no interest in selling hype. Her audience is senior. Treat them accordingly.

**DO:**
- Frame AI as an economic and organisational question, not a technology question
- Use specific numbers: "2,350 hours/month saved", "$12M annually", "500+ resumes/week screened"
- Name real companies (with permission): OKKO, L'Oréal, BNP Paribas
- Take a clear position — "Most AI strategies fail because X" not "AI strategies sometimes struggle"
- Speak to CEOs and boards, not to AI teams or developers
- Use short sentences for emphasis. Then explain with longer ones.
- Start paragraphs with the point, not the setup
- Connect insights to P&L, operating model, governance, or competitive advantage

**DON'T:**
- Open with "In today's rapidly evolving AI landscape..."
- Use: "leverage," "game-changer," "transformative," "cutting-edge," "unlock potential," "harness"
- Ask empty questions at the end: "What do you think? Let me know in the comments!"
- Hedge every statement: "It seems like perhaps AI might potentially..."
- Summarize what everyone already knows without adding Kate's angle
- Write for practitioners or engineers — Kate's audience makes strategic and financial decisions
- Sound like a vendor, a coach, or a conference speaker who hasn't done the work
